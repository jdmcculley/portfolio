Case Study — Dollar General

DG Rewards: Designing a New Deal Type Across the Entire Shopping Funnel

Dollar General was introducing Rewards — a new continuity-based deal program alongside existing coupons and cash back. The challenge wasn't adding another card to the gallery. It was integrating a new deal type across every touchpoint in the shopping experience — from discovery through checkout — across three platforms, without adding cognitive load to a system already serving a billion annual deal clips. I designed the card system, the interaction model, and the full-funnel integration that made Rewards feel native from day one.

01 The Problem

A New Deal Type in a Complex Ecosystem

Dollar General's deals ecosystem was already layered — digital coupons, cash back offers (via Ibotta), and DG Cash all coexisting in a gallery serving 12 million monthly active users. The Cash Back program alone was driving 435 million clips and nearly $9 million earned by customers annually, with 191% year-over-year clip growth. The system was working.

Now the business wanted to introduce Rewards — a new continuity-based deal type where customers earn by purchasing qualifying products over time (buy 3, get $2 back). Unlike one-time-clip coupons, Rewards cards needed to track progress across multiple shopping trips, display real-time status, and coexist alongside existing deal types without confusing customers who were already comfortable with the current model.

The risk was real: add a new deal type poorly and you fragment an experience that millions of customers depend on. The deals gallery, product listing pages, product detail pages, the wallet, and cart/checkout all needed to understand Rewards natively — across App, Mobile Web, and Desktop Web simultaneously.

02 The Approach

Design the Card System First, Then the Funnel

Rather than designing screens for each touchpoint, I started with the card itself. The Rewards card needed to be a self-contained component that could live in any context — a gallery tile, a product page badge, a wallet stack, or a checkout summary — and communicate its state (inactive, partially complete, fully redeemed) at a glance.

I defined three card constructs — Dollar Amount Tracker (spend $30, get $5), Item Tracker (buy 5 items, earn reward), and Trip Tracker (shop 3 trips, unlock savings) — each with distinct progress visualization that made the earn mechanic immediately legible. The card anatomy included vendor branding, savings headline, progress indicator, program icon, and expiration — all governed by a shared spec sheet that merchandising, art departments, and engineering could build from.

With the card system defined, I mapped the full funnel: Deals Gallery → Product Listing Page → Product Detail Page → Wallet → Cart & Checkout. Each touchpoint needed to answer a different question — "What rewards are available?" → "Does this product qualify?" → "How close am I?" → "What am I redeeming?" — and the card system adapted to each context while maintaining visual consistency.

03 Design Decisions

Five Choices That Shaped the System

Decision 01

Component-First Architecture Over Screen-First Design

I could have designed each touchpoint independently — a gallery view, a PDP treatment, a checkout module. Instead, I built a single card component system with context-aware variants. The same Rewards card renders in the gallery (full detail), on a PLP tile (compact badge), in the wallet (stacked), and at checkout (summary). This meant one source of truth for engineering, one set of states to test, and visual consistency customers could rely on across every surface.

Systems Craft
Decision 02

Expandable Card Interaction Over Static Detail Pages

Early explorations used a standard "tap to navigate to detail page" pattern. But in usability considerations, navigating away from the gallery broke the browsing flow — customers lost their scroll position and context. I designed an expanding card drawer that opens inline, showing deal details, qualifying products, and progress tracking without leaving the current view. The card expands with the deal details, product qualifiers, and progress — all within the same context. This kept browsing momentum intact and reduced the tap-to-understand cost by eliminating a full page transition.

Interaction Design
Decision 03

Three Tracker Types With Distinct Progress Visualization

Not all rewards work the same way. A "spend $30" mechanic is fundamentally different from "buy 5 items" or "shop 3 trips." Rather than forcing a generic progress bar, I designed three distinct tracker visualizations: a dollar amount fill for spending thresholds, a punch-card dot system for item counts, and a trip counter for visit-based rewards. Each communicates its earn mechanic at a glance without requiring the customer to read fine print.

Visual Design
Decision 04

Co-Mingled Discovery Over Separate Reward Tabs

The business considered giving Rewards its own section — a separate tab in the deals gallery. Customer research from the Cash Back integration taught us that customers don't think in deal categories. They think in "useful" and "not useful." Rewards cards were co-mingled into the existing deals gallery alongside coupons and cash back, surfaced through the same search, filter, and personalization logic. This meant customers didn't need to learn a new mental model — Rewards appeared naturally where they already looked for deals.

Strategic Decision
Decision 05

Cross-Platform Parity With Platform-Appropriate Patterns

Every screen was designed for three breakpoints: native App, Mobile Web, and Desktop Web. Rather than designing once and scaling, each platform got appropriate interaction patterns — the card drawer uses a native bottom sheet on App, a modal on Mobile Web, and an inline expansion on Desktop. Visual consistency was maintained through shared card components and typography while interaction patterns respected each platform's conventions.

05 Key Learning
"The hardest design problem isn't making something new — it's making something new feel like it was always there. When you integrate a new feature into a system people already trust, the best measure of success is that nobody notices it's new."

Rewards could have been designed as a standalone feature — its own section, its own flow, its own visual language. That would have been faster to ship and easier to scope. But it would have fractured a shopping experience that millions of customers already relied on. The design decision that mattered most wasn't any individual screen — it was the commitment to make Rewards a native citizen of the existing ecosystem, discoverable through the same patterns customers already knew, traceable through the same wallet they already trusted, and redeemable through the same checkout they'd already learned. Integration is harder than invention. But it's what earns trust at scale.
